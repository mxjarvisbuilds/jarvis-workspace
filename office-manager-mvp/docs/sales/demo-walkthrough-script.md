# Office Manager MVP Demo Walkthrough Script

Length: 5–10 minutes  
Audience: Sacramento-area HVAC owner-operator / office lead  
Goal: show recovered revenue, not “AI novelty.”  
Outbound status: **No outbound approved or sent.**

## 0:00–0:45 — Frame the problem
“Most shops don’t lose money because they’re bad at HVAC. They lose it in the gaps: missed calls, estimates that never get followed up, urgent jobs that sit too long, and handoffs where the office, owner, and tech are not looking at the same job context.”

“This is not surveillance AI, not call-recording theater, and not a CRM replacement. It’s a lightweight office-manager layer that starts with the money leak: missed calls and stale estimates.”

## 0:45–2:15 — Missed-call recovery flow
Show the missed-call trigger.

Talk track:
1. “Customer calls. Nobody answers.”
2. “Instead of waiting for voicemail or losing them to the next contractor, they get a fast text from your business.”
3. “The message is simple: acknowledge, collect basics, and route urgency.”
4. “If it is urgent or high-value, it gets kicked to a human. The system stays in its lane.”

Key point:
“Speed-to-lead matters, but so does not sounding like spam. The goal is to make the customer feel answered.”

## 2:15–3:45 — Lead qualification + owner handoff
Show the intake/lead record fields.

Talk track:
“Now the owner or office can see the basics in one place: name, issue, urgency, location, equipment/context, and what the customer wants next. You are not hunting through missed calls, text threads, and memory.”

Differentiator:
“This is where owner-tech alignment starts. The tech does not need another surveillance tool. They need cleaner context before they walk in.”

## 3:45–5:15 — Stale-estimate follow-up flow
Show estimate follow-up sequence.

Talk track:
“Sent estimates are where a lot of money quietly dies. The system follows up in controlled windows until the customer replies, books, says no, or needs a human.”

Example sequence:
- Day 1: helpful check-in
- Day 3: answer-questions / timeline nudge
- Day 7: decision / financing / scheduling nudge
- Stop on reply or opt-out

Key point:
“This is not blasting people forever. It is consistent follow-up where most busy shops drop the ball.”

## 5:15–6:30 — Review protection
Show review-gating concept.

Talk track:
“If the job went well, ask for the review. If the customer is unhappy, route them into private feedback first so the owner can fix it before it becomes public damage.”

## 6:30–8:00 — Expansion path: same job record into field/closeout
Show or explain phase-2 path.

Talk track:
“The first product is revenue recovery. Once that job record is clean, the next layer is simple field execution: dispatch/status, ETA context, job notes, parts/time/photos, and invoice-ready closeout.”

“This matters because the same record can move from lead → estimate → booked job → tech handoff → closeout instead of restarting context at every step.”

## 8:00–9:00 — Offer and guarantee
Talk track:
“The offer is intentionally simple: $199 setup, first month free, then $497/month. Three-month minimum after the free month, with a 60-day money-back guarantee. If it doesn’t recover more revenue than it costs, you should not keep paying for it.”

## 9:00–10:00 — Close / next step
Ask:
“If missed calls or stale estimates are costing you even one job a month, this probably pays for itself. Want me to map this against your current call path and estimate follow-up process?”

Next setup checklist:
- Business number path
- Calendar/booking link or dispatch process
- Service area
- Estimate follow-up rules
- Urgent-job handoff contact
- Review link
- Stop/help compliance copy

## Objection pivots

### “We already have a CRM.”
“Good. I’m not trying to replace it. This fills the gaps most CRMs still leave open: fast missed-call recovery, stale-estimate follow-up, and clean handoff context.”

### “I don’t want AI watching my techs.”
“It doesn’t. This is not surveillance AI. The tech benefit is cleaner job context and fewer chaotic handoffs, not monitoring or grading them.”

### “My office manager handles this.”
“Perfect. This should make them stronger. It handles repetitive timing and reminders so the office manager can focus on judgment calls and urgent jobs.”
